1905 — 1998 · London, England

Tommy Flowers

The Man Who Built the First Computer

Tommy Flowers was a telephone engineer at the General Post Office who built Colossus — the world's first programmable electronic computer — to crack Nazi Germany's high-command communications. The machines helped shorten the war. Then they were destroyed, and Flowers was told never to speak of them. He died largely unknown, thirty years after the secret was declassified.

Computing World War II Cryptography Engineering

A Bricklayer's Son Who Built the Digital Age

Thomas Harold Flowers was born in Poplar, East London, in 1905, the son of a bricklayer. He left school at fourteen and served an apprenticeship in mechanical engineering before joining the General Post Office, where he rose to become one of the leading engineers in the telephone exchange division at Dollis Hill.

By the late 1930s, Flowers had developed an unusual conviction: that electronic valves — vacuum tubes — could be used to build reliable large-scale switching systems. This was considered eccentric. Valves were thought to be too fragile, too hot, and too prone to failure for any device that needed to work continuously. Flowers had quietly discovered that if you never turned them off, they lasted indefinitely.

Bletchley Park and the Lorenz Cipher

In 1940, Flowers was introduced to the codebreakers at Bletchley Park, who were working on two German cipher systems. The Enigma machine was being broken by methods developed by Alan Turing. But there was a second, more complex system called the Lorenz cipher — used by Hitler and his top generals — that required a different approach.

A team at Bletchley had designed an electromechanical machine called Heath Robinson to help with Lorenz decryption. It was slow and unreliable. Flowers proposed replacing it with something entirely electronic. He was told it couldn't be done. He did it anyway, spending his own money on components, working with a small team at Dollis Hill to build Colossus Mark 1, which was delivered to Bletchley Park in December 1943.

"I was told it was not possible to build a reliable machine with 1,500 valves. I knew it was possible. I'd been doing it for years."

Colossus and What It Could Do

Colossus was not a general-purpose computer in the modern sense — it was designed to perform a specific statistical analysis of the Lorenz cipher wheel settings. But it was programmable: its behaviour could be altered by rewiring and resetting its switches, making it the first device in history that combined large-scale electronic computation with programmability.

By the end of the war, ten Colossus machines were running at Bletchley Park. They played a critical role in Operation Overlord, providing intelligence on German dispositions that the Allies used to deceive Hitler about the location of the D-Day landings.

Destruction and Silence

At the end of the war, Winston Churchill ordered the destruction of the Colossus machines. Seven were dismantled. Two were retained by GCHQ, the successor to Bletchley's signals intelligence operation. All documentation was classified. Flowers and his colleagues were told their work was a state secret and they were never to discuss it.

Flowers applied for funding to commercialise what he had learned. He was turned down. When the history of computing was written in the 1950s and 1960s, ENIAC — the American computer completed in 1945 — was described as the first electronic computer. Flowers knew this was wrong. He could not say so.

The Long Wait

The existence of Colossus was partially declassified in 1975, thirty years after the war's end. Flowers was able to speak about his work for the first time when he was seventy years old. He lived until 1998, long enough to see a working replica of Colossus built at Bletchley Park in 2007 — though he died before its completion.

He was appointed MBE in 1943, while the work was still secret. He received no other formal recognition from the British state for building the machine that may have done more than any other single piece of technology to shorten the Second World War.
