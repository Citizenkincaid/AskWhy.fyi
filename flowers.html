Tommy Flowers

The Man Who Built the First Programmable Electronic Computer

Tommy Flowers was a British engineer whose quiet brilliance changed the course of the Second World War. Working in secrecy at Bletchley Park, he designed and built Colossus—the world's first programmable electronic digital computer. His invention helped break Germany's Lorenz cipher and saved countless lives, yet his achievement remained hidden from the public for decades.

Computing World War II Engineering Cryptography

A Bricklayer's Son Who Changed Computing

Thomas Harold Flowers was born in Poplar, East London, in 1905. The son of a bricklayer, he left school at fourteen before serving an apprenticeship in mechanical engineering. His talent eventually led him to the General Post Office, where he became one of Britain's leading telephone engineers at Dollis Hill.

Unlike many engineers of his time, Flowers believed electronic valves could operate continuously without constant failure. While others considered vacuum tubes unreliable, Flowers knew their weakness came from repeatedly switching them on and off. Left running continuously, they were remarkably dependable.

Bletchley Park and a Different Kind of Machine

When Britain entered the Second World War, Flowers was recruited to assist the codebreakers at Bletchley Park. Alan Turing's team had already begun attacking the German Enigma cipher, but another far more sophisticated system—the Lorenz cipher—required an entirely different solution.

The existing machine, Heath Robinson, relied on mechanical parts that frequently fell out of synchronization. Flowers proposed a fully electronic replacement. Most experts believed such a machine would never work. Flowers ignored them.

"I was told it was impossible to build a reliable machine with thousands of valves. I knew it could be done."

Colossus

Working largely with his own initiative—and even contributing some of his own money—Flowers and his team completed Colossus in late 1943. Containing roughly 1,500 vacuum tubes, it became the world's first large-scale programmable electronic digital computer. Unlike later commercial computers, Colossus was designed for one specific purpose: rapidly analysing encrypted Lorenz messages.

By reading encrypted messages at unprecedented speed, Colossus dramatically reduced the time required to break German communications. The intelligence gathered from Lorenz traffic provided the Allied command with invaluable insight into Hitler's plans and military deployments, particularly in the months leading up to the D-Day invasion. By the end of the war, ten Colossus computers were operating at Bletchley Park.

The Secret That Erased History

Victory brought no celebration for Tommy Flowers. Following the war, Winston Churchill ordered nearly every Colossus machine destroyed. Documentation was burned, engineers signed the Official Secrets Act, and Flowers was forbidden from discussing his greatest achievement. For three decades, the world never knew Colossus had existed.

During those years, history credited later American machines such as ENIAC as the world's first electronic computer. Flowers knew that history was incomplete—but he was legally unable to correct it.

A Legacy Finally Revealed

Only in the mid-1970s did the British government begin declassifying information about Colossus. For the first time, Flowers could publicly explain the work he and his colleagues had carried out in total secrecy during the war. Although historians gradually recognised his contribution, the recognition arrived decades after it was deserved.

Today, Colossus is recognised as one of the most important technological achievements of the twentieth century. Its influence stretches across modern computing, cryptography, cybersecurity, and digital engineering. Every programmable computer owes part of its history to Tommy Flowers.
