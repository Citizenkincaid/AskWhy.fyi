1799 — 1847 · Lyme Regis, England

Mary Anning

The Fossil Hunter of Lyme Regis

She discovered some of the most scientifically important fossils of the nineteenth century. She was working class, self-educated, and female — three reasons for the scientific establishment to describe her finds without mentioning her name. She died in 1847. The Geological Society of London did not admit women for another 57 years.

Palaeontology Geology Natural History Science

The Cliffs of Lyme Regis

Mary Anning was born in 1799 in Lyme Regis, a small town on the Dorset coast where the cliffs regularly shed fossils onto the beach below. Her father Richard was a cabinetmaker who supplemented the family's income by selling curiosities — including fossils — to tourists. He taught Mary to find and clean them before dying in 1810, leaving the family in poverty.

Mary was eleven when her father died. She continued fossil hunting to support herself and her mother. The cliffs she worked were dangerous — prone to collapse, exposed to tides — and she worked them for the rest of her life, developing an expertise in the local geology and anatomy that would eventually surpass that of university-trained scientists who came to buy from her.

The Ichthyosaur

In 1811, Mary and her brother Joseph uncovered what appeared to be a large crocodile skull in the cliff face. Mary spent the next year excavating the rest of the skeleton — seventeen feet of bones that turned out to be something no scientist had previously described: an ichthyosaur, a marine reptile from the Jurassic period.

The specimen was bought by a local lord, eventually reached the British Museum, and was described in scientific papers that did not mention Mary Anning's name. This pattern would repeat itself throughout her career. The fossils were extraordinary; the woman who found them was, as far as the published record was concerned, invisible.

"The world has used me so unkindly, I fear it has made me suspicious of everyone."

A Career of Firsts

Over the following decades, Anning found the first complete plesiosaur skeleton, the first pterosaur skeleton discovered outside Germany, and numerous other specimens that reshaped scientists' understanding of prehistoric life. She also made important contributions to the understanding of coprolites — fossilised faeces — recognising their true nature when others dismissed them as curiosities.

Leading geologists and naturalists made pilgrimages to Lyme Regis to meet her and buy from her. She corresponded with scientists across Europe. She read and annotated papers far beyond her formal education. Yet she was never elected to the Geological Society, never credited in most of the papers her finds inspired, and died at 47 of breast cancer, in poverty, supported near the end only by a small annuity that the Geological Society had quietly arranged.

Recognition, Delayed by Decades

In 2010, the Royal Society named Mary Anning one of the ten British women who have most influenced the history of science. In 2022, a natural history museum in Lyme Regis was renamed in her honour. Statues have been erected. A tongue-twister — "she sells seashells by the seashore" — is sometimes said to have been written about her, though the evidence is disputed.

None of this changes what happened. The fossils she found changed how human beings understood the history of life on Earth. Her name was removed from that story by people who thought her gender, class, and lack of credentials made her an unsuitable author of scientific discovery.
