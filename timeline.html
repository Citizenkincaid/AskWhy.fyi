19th Century
1799

Science · Palaeontology

Mary Anning Discovers Her First Ichthyosaur

A twelve-year-old girl in Lyme Regis uncovers a complete ichthyosaur skeleton — the first confirmed specimen in the world. The discovery is reported by male geologists who do not mention her name.

1809

Naval History · Exploration

Cochrane's Fireships at Aix Roads

Captain Thomas Cochrane leads one of the most audacious naval attacks in British history, nearly destroying the French fleet. His admiral refuses to press the advantage. Cochrane is court-martialed within the year.

1836

Medicine · Equality

Elizabeth Garrett Anderson Begins Her Medical Education

Rejected by every medical school in Britain, Anderson teaches herself anatomy, passes the Society of Apothecaries examination, and becomes the first woman licensed to practise medicine in England. The Society promptly changes its rules to exclude women.

1885

Innovation · Engineering

Heaviside Rewrites Maxwell's Equations

Working alone in rented rooms, Oliver Heaviside compresses Maxwell's twenty quaternion equations into the four vector equations still taught in every physics course today. He invents operational calculus, predicts the ionosphere, and is ignored for a decade.

20th Century
1938

Science · Physics

Lise Meitner Explains Nuclear Fission

Lise Meitner provides the theoretical explanation of nuclear fission. The 1944 Nobel Prize in Chemistry goes to her former collaborator Otto Hahn. Meitner is not included, despite being nominated 48 times across her career.

1943

Innovation · Computing

Tommy Flowers Builds Colossus

Post Office engineer Tommy Flowers builds Colossus — the world's first programmable electronic computer — to crack the Lorenz cipher used by Hitler's high command. At the war's end, Churchill orders the machines destroyed. Flowers carries the secret for thirty years.

1972

Medicine · Evidence

Archie Cochrane Publishes Effectiveness and Efficiency

In a slim, clear-eyed book, Archie Cochrane argues that most medical treatments have never been rigorously tested, and that the profession owes its patients evidence — not tradition. The British Medical Journal calls it "a provocation." He is right about nearly everything.
