Hedy Lamarr

The Actress Who Invented the Foundation of WiFi

To the world she was one of the most famous actresses in Hollywood. In secret, during the Second World War, she co-invented a radio guidance system using frequency-hopping spread spectrum technology — the same principle that underlies WiFi, Bluetooth, and GPS today. The patent expired before anyone paid attention. She received no royalties.

Two Lives, One Person

Hedy Lamarr was born Hedwig Eva Maria Kiesler in Vienna in 1914. By her mid-twenties she was under contract at MGM in Hollywood, described by studio executives as "the most beautiful woman in the world," and one of the highest-paid actresses of the 1940s. This is almost everything most people know about her.

What they don't know is that she had been thinking about engineering since childhood, that she had escaped a first marriage to an Austrian arms dealer by disguising herself as a maid, and that she had spent her evenings during the height of her film career working on an invention she believed could help the Allied war effort.

The Problem She Was Trying to Solve

In 1940, German U-boats were sinking Allied ships at a catastrophic rate, partly because radio-controlled torpedoes could be jammed — the enemy simply broadcast on the same frequency as the guidance signal and the torpedo went off course. Lamarr, who had absorbed considerable knowledge of weapons systems from her first husband, understood the problem intuitively.

Her solution, developed with composer George Antheil, was elegant: instead of transmitting on a single frequency, the guidance signal would hop rapidly between frequencies in a pattern known to both the transmitter and the receiver but unpredictable to anyone trying to jam it. You couldn't jam what you couldn't find.

"All creative people want to do the unexpected."

The Patent That Changed the World

Lamarr and Antheil filed for a patent in 1942 under her married name, Hedy Kiesler Markey. The US Navy examined it, found it impractical to implement with the technology available at the time, and shelved it. The patent expired in 1959 — before the technology to make it work had been developed, and before anyone in the industry had paid it serious attention.

In the 1960s, engineers at Sylvania Electronic Systems independently developed spread spectrum technology for use in military satellite communications. The connection to Lamarr and Antheil's patent was noted in technical literature but not widely publicised. By the time the technology found its way into civilian use — WiFi, Bluetooth, CDMA mobile networks, GPS — the patent had long since expired and Lamarr was in her seventies.

Recognition That Came Too Late

In 1997, Lamarr and Antheil were awarded the Electronic Frontier Foundation Pioneer Award. Lamarr, then 82, was reportedly delighted. "It's about time," she said. The same year, she became the first woman to receive the Invention Convention's Inventor of the Year award.

She died in 2000. Her contribution to modern wireless communication is now widely acknowledged — she appears in textbooks, documentaries, and museum exhibitions. The frequency-hopping principle she patented in 1942 is embedded in virtually every wireless device on the planet. She received nothing for it except, eventually, a footnote.
