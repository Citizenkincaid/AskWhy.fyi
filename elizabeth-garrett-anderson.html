1836 — 1917 · London, England

Elizabeth Garrett Anderson

The First Woman Licensed to Practise Medicine in England

Every door was closed to her. Every institution said no. She found the one loophole nobody had thought to close, passed her examinations, and became the first woman licensed to practise medicine in England. Then she built a hospital. Then she became England's first female mayor. She did not wait for permission.

The Doors That Closed

Elizabeth Garrett was born in Whitechapel, London, in 1836, the daughter of a successful pawnbroker. In 1860, aged 24, she decided she wanted to become a doctor. This was not something women did. No British medical school would admit her. The Society of Apothecaries, which could grant a licence to practise medicine, had never considered the possibility of a female applicant and had no explicit rule against one.

Garrett found a physician willing to let her study privately and attend some lectures at Middlesex Hospital. She was eventually barred from the hospital after male students objected to her presence. She sat the Society of Apothecaries examination in 1865, passed, and was granted her licence. The Society immediately changed its rules to prevent other women from doing the same.

The New Hospital for Women

Unable to obtain a hospital appointment because of her sex, Garrett opened her own dispensary for women and children in London in 1866. She paid for it largely herself. It became the New Hospital for Women, staffed entirely by women, and eventually the Elizabeth Garrett Anderson Hospital — which operated until 2001, when it merged with University College Hospital.

In 1870, Garrett obtained her MD from the University of Paris — the only institution that would award her one. In the same year, she was elected to the London School Board, one of the first women elected to public office in Britain. She helped found the London School of Medicine for Women in 1874 and served as its dean.

"We are human beings in the same world, needing the same food, air, and exercise, and the same moral and intellectual nourishment."

A Life of Firsts

In 1908, aged 72, Elizabeth Garrett Anderson was elected Mayor of Aldeburgh in Suffolk — the first female mayor in English history. She had by this point been a practising physician for over forty years, had helped establish women's medical education in Britain, and had outlived most of the institutions that had refused to admit her.

She died in 1917, at 81. The British Medical Association, which had admitted her as its first female member in 1873 and then promptly changed its rules to exclude women for the next 19 years, eventually named a ward after her. The hospital she founded bore her name until it closed. The medical school she helped create still trains doctors today.

What She Made Possible

When Elizabeth Garrett Anderson began her medical career, no woman in England held a medical licence. By the time she died, women were studying medicine at universities across the country. She had not waited for attitudes to change. She had found the gaps in the rules, walked through them, and built institutions on the other side that made it harder to close the door again.
