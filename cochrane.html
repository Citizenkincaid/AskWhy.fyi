Archie Cochrane

The Man Who Asked Medicine to Prove It

A Scottish epidemiologist who spent his career demanding that medical treatments be tested before being trusted. His insistence on randomised controlled trials transformed medicine — after decades of resistance from a profession that preferred authority to evidence.

Medicine Epidemiology Evidence Public Health

A Doctor Shaped by War

Archibald Leman Cochrane was born in Galashiels, Scotland, in 1909. He studied medicine at Cambridge and University College London, but it was his experience as a prisoner of war that defined his thinking. Captured in Crete in 1941, Cochrane spent four years in German and Italian prisoner-of-war camps, where he served as the medical officer for thousands of fellow prisoners with almost no supplies.

Working under those conditions, Cochrane was forced to question everything he thought he knew about medicine. He had no drugs, no equipment, and no way to apply most of what he had been taught. What he did have was careful observation — and the growing suspicion that many medical interventions were either useless or actively harmful.

The Randomised Controlled Trial

After the war, Cochrane became a champion of the randomised controlled trial as the only reliable way to know whether a medical treatment actually worked. This was not a popular position. Medicine at the time operated largely on the authority of senior clinicians — if an experienced doctor believed a treatment worked, that was considered sufficient.

Cochrane argued that this was not good enough. Patients deserved treatments that had been tested against placebos, in properly designed trials, with results that could be replicated. He conducted several landmark trials himself, including one that showed — controversially — that home care for heart attack patients was no worse than hospital care, potentially saving enormous resources.

"It is surely a great criticism of our profession that we have not organised a critical summary, by speciality or subspeciality, of all relevant randomised controlled trials."

Effectiveness and Efficiency

In 1972, Cochrane published a short, blunt book called Effectiveness and Efficiency: Random Reflections on Health Services. In it, he argued that the National Health Service was spending enormous sums on treatments that had never been properly tested, and that a systematic review of the evidence for every medical intervention was urgently needed.

The book was not well received by the medical establishment. The British Medical Journal described it as a provocation. Several professional organisations condemned it. Cochrane was accused of arrogance and of undermining clinical judgement. He was, in almost every particular, correct.

The Cochrane Collaboration

Cochrane died in 1988, before he could see the full impact of his work. But in 1993, five years after his death, the Cochrane Collaboration was founded in his honour — an international network dedicated to producing systematic reviews of medical evidence. It now maintains over 8,000 reviews covering virtually every area of medicine, and has become one of the most important resources in healthcare worldwide.

The randomised controlled trial is now the gold standard of medical research. Systematic reviews are required before new treatments are approved in most countries. The infrastructure of modern evidence-based medicine exists because one obstinate Scottish doctor refused to accept that authority was a substitute for proof.
