Curiosity-driven essays that ask why credit disappears, why genius goes unrecognised, and what structures make that possible.

Attribution

Why Does Credit Flow Upward?

The Matthew Effect in science: those who already have status receive credit for discoveries made by those who don't. An exploration of how citation patterns, Nobel committees, and academic hierarchy all push recognition toward names we already know.

Gender & Science

Why Were Women's Contributions to Science Systematically Removed?

From Rosalind Franklin to Lise Meitner to Nettie Stevens, a pattern repeats. Women perform key research. Men receive the prize. This essay traces the mechanisms — institutional, social, and procedural — that made erasure routine.

Secrecy & Memory

What Happens to History When Governments Classify It?

Tommy Flowers built the world's first programmable computer. He was told never to speak of it. The history of computing was written without him. When the files were declassified, it was too late for most people to revise what they thought they knew.

Class & Credentialism

Why Did Oliver Heaviside's Ideas Take Twenty Years to Be Accepted?

Heaviside had no university education, no institutional affiliation, and no patron. His papers were correct. The scientific establishment had several reasons to ignore them, and only one reason to accept them: they were right.

Evidence & Resistance

Why Did Medicine Resist Evidence for So Long?

Archie Cochrane asked doctors to prove their treatments worked. The resistance he encountered reveals something important about professions, authority, and the gap between what we know and what we do.

Narrative & History

Who Decides Which Stories Get Told?

History is not a record. It is a selection. This essay examines who controlled the selection process in science and engineering for two centuries, and what that means for what we think we know about how the world was built.
